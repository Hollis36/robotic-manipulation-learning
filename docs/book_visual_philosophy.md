# Book Visual Philosophy: Kinematic Atlas

Kinematic Atlas treats robotic manipulation as a mapped field of forces, frames, and decisions. Space is organized like a technical wall chart: large calm regions, precise geometric structures, and small annotated marks that reward slow inspection. The visual language should feel meticulously crafted, with every line and node placed by deliberate measurement rather than decoration.

The palette is restrained but not monotone. Cool blue, teal, vermillion, amber, graphite, and quiet paper-grey share the work so no single hue dominates. Color acts as an information system: blue for robot state and geometry, teal for perception and environment, vermillion for constraints and contact, amber for goals and rewards. The result should show painstaking attention to chromatic balance.

Forms are abstract but grounded in mechanisms. A reader should sense robot arms, camera cones, point clouds, grippers, planners, controllers, and policies without needing literal illustration. Repeated circles, frames, paths, grids, and arrows create rhythm. The work should look like the product of deep expertise: a master-level diagram that can teach before the text explains.

Typography stays sparse. Words act as quiet labels, never paragraphs. The image must communicate through composition first: scale, proximity, alignment, and contrast carry the meaning. Text is included only when it anchors a subsystem or marks a transition.

Every figure should feel labored over with care. The images are not decorative banners; they are study artifacts. Each page figure should look useful enough for a notebook, polished enough for a public GitHub book, and disciplined enough to sit beside engineering content without reducing it to clip art.
